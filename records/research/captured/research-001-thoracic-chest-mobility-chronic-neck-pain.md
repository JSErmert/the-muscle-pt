## L1 — Source Capture

### Citation

Wirth B, Amstalden M, Perk M, Boutellier U, Humphreys BK. The relation between thoracic spine mobility, chest expansion, respiratory function, and chronic non-specific neck pain. Manual Therapy. 2014.

PMID: 24835338 | https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24835338/

### Study Design

Cross-sectional comparative study. Examined relationships between thoracic spine mobility, chest expansion, respiratory function, and chronic non-specific neck pain (CNP).

### Population

Chronic non-specific neck pain patients vs. healthy controls.

### Variables Measured

- Thoracic spine mobility (flexion, extension)
- Chest expansion
- Maximum voluntary ventilation (MVV)
- Maximum expiratory pressure (Pemax)
- Maximum inspiratory pressure (Pimax)
- Neck Disability Index (NDI)

### Key Results — exact figures from abstract

- Thoracic spine mobility during flexion correlated significantly with MVV: **r = 0.45**
- Chest expansion correlated significantly with MVV: **r = 0.42**
- Pemax was related to NDI: **r = -0.58**
- Pimax was related to NDI: **r = -0.46**

### Stated Conclusion

Thoracic mobility and chest expansion are associated with respiratory function in CNP. Respiratory pressures (Pemax, Pimax) inversely correlate with neck disability — greater disability, weaker respiratory pressure generation.

## L2 — Insight

### What This Establishes

Chronic neck pain is not purely a cervical pathology. Wirth demonstrates measurable, statistically significant associations between cervical disability and adjacent thoracic + respiratory structures. The stronger the disability (NDI), the weaker the respiratory pressures — consistent with a compensatory cascade where chronic neck dysfunction co-occurs with restricted thoracic mobility and reduced chest expansion.

The cervicothoracic junction sits at the literal anatomical center of every variable Wirth measured. Anything that stiffens that junction degrades all four downstream measures (thoracic flexion mobility, chest expansion, MVV, respiratory pressure generation).

### Provocation / Relief Pair — from linked real case

The mechanical axis Wirth's variables describe is the same axis the linked case is centered on:

- **Provocation:** Sustained thoracic flexion + scapular protraction + forward head load (bowl-shaped car seat, prolonged forward-leaning posture) → posterior chain pain wrap, C-T junction inflammation flare, worst episodes of agony
- **Relief:** Thoracic extension + scapular retraction (arm behind back, lean back into surface, squeeze posterior shoulder region) → C-T junction unloads, pain reduces

Provocation = restricted thoracic mobility in flexion + reduced chest expansion (the exact pattern Wirth showed degrades respiratory and cervical function).
Relief = restored thoracic mobility in extension + scapular repositioning.

This is not coincidence. It is Wirth's correlations expressed clinically in a single human.

### What This Does NOT Establish

- Wirth measured correlation, not causation. Direction of the relationship is not proven.
- Sample is non-specific CNP — findings do not extend to:
  - cervical radiculopathy / nerve compression patterns (deferred to research-002)
  - chronic inflammation / autoimmune flare cycles (deferred to research-003)
  - post-dislocation scapulothoracic instability (deferred to research-004)
- Does not address pediatric, post-surgical, or post-traumatic acute populations
- Single-study evidence — replication not yet captured
