## L1 — Source Capture

### Citation

García-Juez S, Navarro-Santana MJ, Valera-Calero JA, Albert-Lucena D, Varas-de-la-Fuente AB, Plaza-Manzano G. Effectiveness of Articular and Neural Mobilization for Managing Cervical Radicular Pain: A Systematic Review With Network Meta-Analysis. Journal of Orthopaedic & Sports Physical Therapy. 2025.

PMID: 40576779 | https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40576779/

### Study Design

**Network meta-analysis with systematic review.** Synthesizes RCT evidence across multiple intervention combinations and ranks them on effect sizes for pain intensity and pain-related disability in cervical radicular pain.

### Population

Patients with cervical radicular pain. Includes the chronic non-specific cervical radiculopathy populations that align with research-002's case context.

### Sample Size

- **777 reports screened**
- **50 studies analyzed quantitatively** in the network meta-analysis
- Total participant count not specified in abstract; defer to full text

### Variables Measured

- **Pain intensity** — short-term outcome (mean difference units)
- **Pain-related disability** — standardized mean difference units (SMD scale, comparable across instruments such as NDI)

### Intervention Categories Compared

- **Articular + neural mobilization with usual care**
- **Neural mobilization with usual care**
- **Usual care alone** (standard care)
- **Wait and see / sham / placebo controls**

### Key Results — exact figures from abstract

**Pain intensity (short-term):**
- **Articular + neural mobilization with usual care vs. wait/see/sham/placebo: MD -3.23 (95% CI: -4.33, -2.12)**
- **Articular + neural mobilization with usual care vs. standard care alone: MD -1.52 (95% CI: -2.31, -0.73)**

**Pain-related disability:**
- **Neural mobilization plus usual care vs. wait/see/sham/placebo: SMD -1.57 (95% CI: -2.53, -0.61)** — large effect by Cohen's conventions
- **Neural mobilization plus usual care vs. usual care alone: SMD -1.31 (95% CI: -1.88, -0.73)** — large effect

By Cohen's conventions: SMD ≥ 0.8 = large effect. Both disability SMDs sit well above this threshold (1.31, 1.57), with the lower bounds of confidence intervals still in meaningful-effect territory (0.61–0.73).

### Stated Conclusion

The combination of articular and neural mobilization with usual care was most effective in reducing short-term pain intensity compared to wait/see, sham, or placebo interventions. Significant improvements in pain-related disability with neural mobilization plus usual care, surpassing wait/see, sham, placebo, and usual care alone.

The authors note risk of bias and heterogeneity downgrade certainty of evidence; combining mobilization with standard care should be considered with care due to moderate to very low certainty across some comparisons.

## L2 — Insight

### What This Establishes

**Conservative articular and neural mobilization produces large, clinically meaningful effects on both pain and disability in cervical radicular pain at network-meta-analysis level.** Three independent claims emerge:

1. **The combined intervention beats no-treatment controls on pain by clinically meaningful margins** (MD -3.23, with -4.33 lower bound)
2. **The combined intervention beats standard care alone on pain** (MD -1.52, with -2.31 lower bound) — adding mobilization to standard care produces additional benefit
3. **Neural mobilization produces large effects on disability** (SMD -1.57 vs. controls; -1.31 vs. usual care alone) — both comparisons in large-effect territory

The clinical translation: **for chronic cervical radicular pain without red flags, conservative articular and neural mobilization is evidenced as a meaningful treatment**, not a placebo-equivalent. The 6-week window from research-002 is consistent with the durations evidenced in García-Juez's component RCTs (typical 4–8 week windows in this literature).

### What This Does NOT Establish

- **Specific dosing/protocol details:** García-Juez does not specify intervention frequency, intensity, duration at NMA level — these vary across the 50 component studies. Practical dosing requires drilling into specific RCTs (research-002's 12 sessions over 6 weeks is one such anchor)
- **Long-term outcomes:** the abstract emphasizes short-term pain effects. Long-term durability is not addressed at the network meta-analysis level reported in the abstract
- **Imaging-based outcomes:** Research-005 measures clinical pain/disability outcomes, not biological markers. Research-002 remains the only record with imaging-quantified mechanism evidence — this gap is acknowledged
- **Heterogeneity of populations:** "cervical radicular pain" includes acute, subacute, and chronic populations across the included RCTs. Research-002 specifically addressed chronic. Generalization across the chronicity spectrum should be done conservatively
- **Intervention substitution:** Research-005 covers articular + neural mobilization. Substituting other manual therapy approaches (manipulation, IASTM, dry needling, etc.) is not evidenced by this record — different intervention classes need separate evidence
