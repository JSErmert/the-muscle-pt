# RESEARCH RECORD 008 — Hypermobility / Asymmetric Laxity Shoulder Instability Rehabilitation

This file holds Layers 1 and 2 (source capture + insight). Layer 3 (system mapping) is held at `records/research/mapped/research-008-hypermobility-shoulder-instability-rehabilitation.md`.

This record is **structurally different from research-001 through research-007.** It is a **horizontal modifier** that applies across other records — when a case has hypermobility features, this record's principles modify how the other records' interventions should be applied. It is analogous in position to red-flag screening: a constitutional/contextual factor that screens *before* mechanism-specific sequencing is applied.

## L1 — Source Capture

### Citation

Spanhove V, De Wandele I, Malfait F, Calders P, Cools A. Home-based exercise therapy for treating shoulder instability in patients with hypermobile Ehlers-Danlos syndrome/hypermobility spectrum disorders. A randomized trial. Disability & Rehabilitation. 2023.

PMID: 35609204 | https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35609204/

### Study Design

**Randomized Controlled Trial.** Two-arm comparison of two home-based exercise programs in patients with hEDS/HSD and multidirectional shoulder instability.

### Population

**21 hEDS/HSD patients with multidirectional shoulder instability.** This is the directly relevant population for the linked case's constitutional substrate (unilateral right-side hyperlaxity + recurrent right scapular dislocation history).

### Sample Size

- **N = 21** (small sample — typical for the hypermobility-specific shoulder instability literature)

### Intervention

- **6-month home-based exercise program**
- Two arms compared (group A and group B); abstract does not detail program content differences
- No significant between-group difference was found — within-group improvements are the operative findings

### Assessment Time Points

Baseline, 6 weeks, 12 weeks, 24 weeks.

### Variables Measured

- **WOSI (Western Ontario Shoulder Instability Index)** — primary disease-specific measure
- **DASH (Disabilities of the Arm, Shoulder and Hand)** — functional measure
- **PSFS (Patient-Specific Functional Scale)** — patient-reported function
- **GROC (Global Rating of Change)** — patient-reported global change
- **TSK (Tampa Scale of Kinesiophobia)** — fear-of-movement measure

### Key Results — exact figures from abstract

- **WOSI improvement: 240 points at 12 weeks (p = 0.02); 325 points at 24 weeks (p = 0.001)** — clinically meaningful magnitude
- **DASH: 8.6 point improvement at 24 weeks (p = 0.002)**
- **PSFS: 4.3 point improvement at 24 weeks (p = 0.01)**
- **GROC: 1.02 point improvement at 24 weeks (p = 0.001)**
- **TSK: no significant effect (p = 0.12)** — kinesiophobia (fear of movement) was not modified by the intervention
- **No significant between-group differences** — both home-based programs produced the within-group improvements

### Stated Conclusion

Both home-based exercise programs produced clinically meaningful improvements in shoulder function and pain in hEDS/HSD patients with multidirectional shoulder instability over 24 weeks, with no significant difference between specific program variants. Fear of movement (kinesiophobia) was not significantly modified by either program — suggesting that fear-of-movement requires a different intervention class.

## L2 — Insight

### What This Establishes

**Home-based exercise therapy produces clinically meaningful shoulder instability improvement in hypermobile patients over 6 months.** The WOSI improvement trajectory (240 → 325 points across 12–24 weeks) demonstrates progressive within-group improvement, with multiple secondary outcome measures (DASH, PSFS, GROC) all confirming clinically significant gains. Spanhove provides the **first RCT-level evidence specifically for shoulder instability rehabilitation in the hEDS/HSD population**.

The within-group results are the operative findings since the two-arm comparison showed no between-group difference. This actually strengthens the practical takeaway: **multiple home-based exercise approaches produce comparable improvement** — the specific protocol is less critical than the structured progression itself, with consistent home-based programming over 6 months.

### Direct Mapping to the Linked Case

The linked case (query-001 v5 + query-008) has the textbook constitutional substrate that Spanhove studied:

- **Multidirectional instability features:** two prior right scapular dislocations consistent with multidirectional capsular laxity (vs. typical unidirectional Bankart lesion presentations)
- **Underlying laxity:** right-side body laxity (right shoulder blade, right jaw, right upper trap explicitly identified)
- **Compensatory pattern:** right upper trap overactivity is the textbook compensatory response to underlying lax shoulder mechanics
- **Chronic state:** the condition is stable-but-chronic, not acute-reducible

Spanhove's intervention class (home-based exercise over 6 months) is directly portable as the conservative trial framework for the linked case. Expected trajectory: WOSI improvement on the order of 240 points by 12 weeks, 325 points by 24 weeks. Cases substantially below this trajectory at 12-week reassessment warrant escalation evaluation.

### Critical Position — Horizontal Modifier Across Records

Research-008 is the **first horizontal modifier** in the system. Rather than introducing a new mechanism layer or case category, it modifies how *every other applicable record's intervention* should be applied to hypermobile cases:

| Other Record | Hypermobility-Aware Modification |
|---|---|
| research-001 (mechanical sequencing) | Mobility/stretching-first staging is contraindicated for hypermobile cases — joint-position-sense and stability work come first; mobility work is gentler, eccentric-biased, position-controlled |
| research-002 (neural tissue management) | Progression is gentler; avoid forced end-range neural tensioning; emphasize joint position sense alongside neurodynamic work |
| research-003 (combined-modality CS programming) | Combined program emphasis shifts toward strengthening + proprioception over stretching; the Ibrahim "stretching alone insufficient" finding is reinforced |
| research-004 (post-dislocation scapular rehab) | Carbone's framework directly applies; many AC dislocations occur in hypermobile populations — the populations overlap |
| research-005 (neural mobilization for cervical radicular pain) | Neural mobilization is gentler; usual care components emphasize stability work |
| research-006 (TMJ — planned) | When authored, hypermobile TMJ cases need stability-emphasis approach (avoid forced end-range opening) |
| research-007 (broader post-dislocation — planned) | When authored, the broader population evidence will likely confirm hypermobility prevalence in recurrent dislocators |

**Cross-record principle (the operating modification across all hypermobile cases):**

> Stability over flexibility. Proprioception over passive range. Slow eccentric over end-range stretching. Closed-chain over open-chain in early phases. Compensatory muscle downregulation must be paired with restoration of underlying stability — never stretch the overactive compensator without addressing the laxity it is compensating for.

### What This Does NOT Establish

- **Not a Beighton scoring or diagnostic criterion source** — Spanhove studied an already-diagnosed hEDS/HSD population. Diagnostic differentiation between asymmetric laxity, generalized hypermobility, hEDS, and HSD requires separate clinical assessment instruments
- **Single study, n=21** — cannot establish population-scale generalizability the way research-003 (89 RCTs) or research-005 (50 studies) can. Confidence is Medium with explicit acknowledgment
- **Two-arm comparison with no between-group winner** — does not validate one specific program over another; only validates the broader category of home-based exercise
- **No sham/no-treatment control** — cannot rule out regression to the mean or natural history at the population level (though 6-month follow-up argues for sustained effect)
- **Fear-of-movement (TSK) was not modified** — psychological factors in hypermobility cases need separate intervention; exercise alone is insufficient for this dimension
- **Does not specify proprioceptive vs. strengthening vs. stability components** — the abstract treats the program as a unit; full-text review needed for prescription detail
- **Adult hEDS/HSD population** — does not extend to pediatric hypermobility, generalized non-hEDS hypermobility without instability, or post-traumatic acquired laxity

### Supplementary Context (cited but not seed)

- **PMID 37231592** (Brittain et al. 2024, Disability and Rehabilitation) — scoping review, 28 articles, 630 participants. Confirms therapeutic exercise and motor function training are efficacious for G-HSD/hEDS at population level. No specific effect sizes in abstract. Useful as ecosystem-level supporting context.
- **PMID 34145717** (Reychler et al. 2021, AJMG-A) — systematic review, 6 RCTs, n=20-57, programs 4–8 weeks. "Pain or proprioception demonstrated significant improvements in the intervention group." Useful as supplementary context confirming the broader hEDS PT literature.

These two are flagged here for L3 context only and explicitly not used as primary citation grounding.

### Confidence Level

**Medium.** Drivers:
- RCT design (rigorous individual-study evidence level)
- Direct case-population match (hEDS/HSD with multidirectional shoulder instability)
- Multiple validated outcome measures with verifiable exact figures
- 6-month home-based intervention is clinically practical
- BUT: single study, n=21, no between-group winner, no sham control, single journal

Confidence promotion path to High:
- A meta-analysis or NMA on hypermobility-specific shoulder instability rehab (does not appear to exist yet at the level of research-003 or research-005)
- ≥ 1 dual-stream Phase 2 hypermobile case demonstrating WOSI improvement trajectory consistent with Spanhove (clinical replication)
- Larger RCTs with sham controls (would address current methodological gap)

This is the practical reality of the hypermobility-specific literature — sample sizes are small, and meta-analyses are scoping/qualitative rather than NMA-level. Spanhove is the strongest available seed despite its limitations.
