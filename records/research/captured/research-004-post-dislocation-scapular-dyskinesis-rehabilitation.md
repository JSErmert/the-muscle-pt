# RESEARCH RECORD 004 — Post-Dislocation Scapular Dyskinesis Rehabilitation

This file holds Layers 1 and 2 (source capture + insight). Layer 3 (system mapping) is held at `records/research/mapped/research-004-post-dislocation-scapular-dyskinesis-rehabilitation.md`.

## L1 — Source Capture

### Citation

Carbone S, Postacchini R, Gumina S. Scapular dyskinesis and SICK syndrome in patients with a chronic type III acromioclavicular dislocation. Results of rehabilitation. Knee Surgery, Sports Traumatology, Arthroscopy. 2015.

PMID: 24458335 | https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24458335/

### Study Design

Retrospective case series with prospective follow-up measurements. **Level of Evidence: IV.**

### Population

Patients with chronic **type III acromioclavicular (AC) dislocation** with documented scapular dyskinesis, treated with structured conservative rehabilitation. Chronic post-dislocation state (not acute), no surgical intervention.

### Sample Size

- **24 patients enrolled**
- **23 analyzed at 12-month follow-up** (1 patient lost to follow-up)
- **14 of 24 had SICK syndrome** at baseline (SICK = Scapular malposition, Inferior medial border prominence, Coracoid pain and malposition, dysKinesis of scapular movement)

### Intervention

**12 strengthening and stretching exercises of the scapulae.** Specific exercise components and dosing parameters not detailed in abstract — defer to full text for precise prescription.

### Follow-Up

Three measurement time points: **6 weeks, 6 months, and 12 months** post-intervention initiation.

### Variables Measured

- Scapular dyskinesis (presence/absence by clinical observation)
- SICK Scapula Rating Scale (quantitative)
- Constant Score (validated shoulder functional outcome measure)
- Subjective Shoulder Value (SSV — patient-reported functional measure, 0–100 scale)

### Key Results — exact figures from abstract

- **Scapular dyskinesis resolved in 18/23 patients (78.2%) at 12 months**
- **SICK syndrome observed in 4/8 patients with scapular malposition** post-treatment
- **SICK Scapula Rating Scale: 7.5 points** in 4 patients post-treatment
- **Mean Constant Score and Subjective Shoulder Value: improved to 85 points** at 12 months
- **Level of Evidence: IV**

### Stated Conclusion

Structured scapular rehabilitation (12 exercises over a multi-month program) produces resolution of scapular dyskinesis in approximately 78% of chronic type III AC dislocation patients at 12 months, with substantial improvement in functional outcomes (Constant Score, SSV).

## L2 — Insight

### What This Establishes

**Chronic post-dislocation scapular dyskinesis is responsive to structured rehabilitation at the population level.** The 78.2% resolution figure at 12 months is the kind of practical clinical evidence post-dislocation case management needs:

- Most chronic post-dislocation scapular dysfunction can resolve without surgery
- The intervention timeline that works is months, not weeks (6/12 month checkpoints)
- The intervention is specific (12 scapular-focused exercises), not generic shoulder rehab
- Functional outcomes (Constant Score, SSV improving to 85 points) corroborate the kinematic resolution

The mechanism this validates: **post-dislocation event → altered scapular kinematics + capsular/ligamentous laxity → compensatory muscle splinting → chronic scapular dyskinesis → responsive to targeted scapular-focused rehabilitation.**

### Direct Mapping to the Linked Case

The query-004 case has the post-dislocation history that Carbone's population shares:

- **Two prior dislocations at the right shoulder blade region** in the linked case
- Right shoulder blade region remains a primary symptomatic site — likely involves chronic scapular dyskinesis and compensatory splinting per the Carbone mechanism
- The query-001 provocation/relief patterns are *especially pronounced* at the right shoulder blade region — consistent with chronic scapular dysfunction amplifying mechanical sensitivity
- The 12-week structured-rehabilitation framework provides a directly portable conservative trial window

### Important Limitation — AC Specificity

**Carbone's population is type III acromioclavicular (AC) dislocation specifically — not glenohumeral, scapulothoracic, or other shoulder dislocation types.** The linked case has anatomical ambiguity ("right shoulder blade region — scapulothoracic / glenohumeral region — exact diagnosis not yet specified"):

| Dislocation type | Mechanism | Carbone applies? |
|---|---|---|
| Acromioclavicular (AC) | AC ligament disruption → altered scapular suspension | **Direct evidence** |
| Glenohumeral | Capsular/labral disruption → altered humeral-scapular kinematics | Inferred — scapular dyskinesis is downstream consequence; rehab response plausible but not directly evidenced by Carbone |
| Scapulothoracic | Scapular instability primary | Inferred — scapular-focused rehab is anatomically appropriate; rehab response plausible but not directly evidenced |

Without clinical workup of the linked case, we don't know which type applies. Carbone provides direct evidence for AC; reasonable inference for glenohumeral and scapulothoracic. The L3 system mapping should treat this as "direct evidence in AC, inferential in other types — flag for conservative interpretation until research-007 supplies broader-population evidence."

### Cross-Record Interaction

For the linked case, research-004 likely **interacts with research-003** in a meaningful way: chronic post-dislocation peripheral nociceptive input (Carbone's mechanism) drives chronic peripheral sensitization → upregulates central sensitization (Ibrahim's mechanism) → amplifies the regional spreading and burning pattern. Treating only the central sensitization without addressing the upstream peripheral driver (chronic scapular instability) may produce incomplete results. The reverse is also true: treating only the scapular dysfunction without addressing the central sensitization may leave the regional symptom pattern intact even after scapular kinematics resolve.

This argues for **parallel addressing of both layers** in cases that present with both — consistent with research-003's RC-9 sequencing principle.

### What This Does NOT Establish

- **AC specificity:** findings do not directly extend to glenohumeral, scapulothoracic, or other dislocation types — research-007 (planned) addresses this gap
- **Acute vs. chronic:** Carbone studies chronic post-event populations. Acute reducible dislocations require immediate medical reduction — different management
- **High-frequency recurrent dislocation:** Carbone's cohort is chronic, not high-frequency recurrent. Cases with multiple dislocations per year may need surgical evaluation — not extended conservative trial
- **Specific anatomical lesions:** Findings do not modify expectations for cases with imaged lesions (large Hill-Sachs, significant Bankart, posterior labral tear, etc.) — these may have lower rehabilitation response rates
- **Specific exercise prescription:** abstract specifies "12 exercises" but does not detail content, frequency, intensity, or progression — defer to full-text review
