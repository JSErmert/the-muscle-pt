## Case Engine Mapping

### Hypermobility Screen — New Case Engine Step

A **constitutional screen** is added to the case engine, applied **before** any mechanism-specific sequencing per research-001 through research-007:

**Hypermobility screen indicators:**
- Beighton score ≥ 5/9 (validated generalized hypermobility threshold)
- Recurrent joint dislocations (especially multidirectional or atraumatic)
- Asymmetric / unilateral hyperlaxity (uncommon presentation; warrants careful evaluation)
- Compensatory muscle overactivity in regions adjacent to lax joints (e.g., upper trap dominance with shoulder laxity, suboccipital tightness with cervical laxity)
- Family history of hypermobility / hEDS
- Multi-system features suggesting underlying connective tissue disorder (autonomic dysregulation, GI motility issues, easy bruising) — refer for medical specialist if present

**If screen positive:** apply hypermobility-aware modifications to all relevant record interventions per research-008's principles.

**If screen negative:** apply mechanism-specific records as designed (research-001/002/003/004/005 standard sequencing).

**If screen ambiguous:** treat as cautiously hypermobility-aware until formal Beighton scoring or clinical assessment clarifies. The cost of erring toward stability emphasis is low; the cost of aggressive end-range work in an undiagnosed hypermobile case is high (further capsular stress, recurrent dislocation risk).

### Reassessment Infrastructure — Hypermobility-Specific

Spanhove supplies validated outcome measures and expected trajectory anchors:

- **WOSI (Western Ontario Shoulder Instability Index)** — primary disease-specific measure. Expected improvement trajectory: ~240 points at 12 weeks, ~325 points at 24 weeks
- **DASH (Disabilities of the Arm, Shoulder and Hand)** — functional outcome (~8.6 point improvement at 24 weeks)
- **PSFS (Patient-Specific Functional Scale)** — patient-reported function (~4.3 point improvement at 24 weeks)
- **GROC (Global Rating of Change)** — perceived improvement (~1.02 point improvement at 24 weeks)

**TSK (Tampa Scale of Kinesiophobia)** is *not* expected to improve with exercise alone — fear-of-movement requires separate cognitive/educational intervention (this is itself a useful finding: don't expect exercise to fix kinesiophobia).

Field-usable subset (no equipment needed): **PSFS + GROC**. WOSI requires the validated questionnaire (free, available online). DASH similarly available.

### Empirical Anchor for Conservative Trial in Hypermobile Cases

Spanhove's intervention parameters as a portable trial framework:

- **Duration:** 6 months minimum (24 weeks), with checkpoints at 6, 12, and 24 weeks
- **Content:** structured home-based exercise program (specific protocol detail requires full-text review; the broad category of home-based progressive exercise produces the documented effects)
- **Expected trajectory:** progressive within-group improvement across WOSI, DASH, PSFS, GROC
- **Decision points:**
  - Week 12 reassessment: if WOSI tracking toward ~240 point improvement, continue
  - Week 24 reassessment: if WOSI tracking toward ~325 point improvement, the trial is succeeding
  - Cases substantially below trajectory at 12-week reassessment warrant escalation evaluation
  - Plateau before 24 weeks despite trajectory match earlier may signal need for program progression or differential reconsideration

## Decision Mapping

### Hypermobility-Aware Modifications Across Records

For cases positive on the hypermobility screen, modify other records' interventions per these principles:

#### research-001 (Postural-Mechanical Sequencing)

- **Standard:** mobility-first → chest expansion → scapular repositioning → cervical loading
- **Hypermobility-modified:** **stability and proprioception first**, then mobility work as a secondary phase, with all mobility work being **eccentric-biased and position-controlled** (no aggressive end-range stretching)

#### research-002 (Neural Tissue Management)

- **Standard:** 6-week / 12-session nerve and joint mobilisation
- **Hypermobility-modified:** gentler progression; avoid forced end-range neural tensioning; emphasize joint position sense alongside neurodynamic technique; the same 6-week framework applies but with reduced aggression

#### research-003 (Combined-Modality CS Programming)

- **Standard:** combined stretching + strengthening (or stretching + strengthening + aerobic) for CS-positive cases
- **Hypermobility-modified:** combined program emphasis **shifts toward strengthening + proprioception**, with stretching as a minor component (not absent, but reduced); Ibrahim's "stretching alone insufficient" finding is reinforced for hypermobile populations

#### research-004 (Post-Dislocation Scapular Rehabilitation)

- **Standard:** 12-week structured scapular-focused rehabilitation (12 strengthening + stretching exercises)
- **Hypermobility-modified:** Carbone's framework directly applies — many AC dislocations occur in hypermobile populations. The 12-exercise scapular framework works as designed. Add proprioceptive emphasis and ensure stretching components don't push end-range.

#### research-005 (Neural Mobilization for Cervical Radicular Pain)

- **Standard:** articular + neural mobilization with usual care
- **Hypermobility-modified:** mobilization is gentler; usual care components emphasize stability work over passive mobility; no end-range neural tensioning

### Decision Logic — When Hypermobility Screen Is Positive

1. **Always apply the cross-record modifier principles** (stability over flexibility, proprioception over passive range, slow eccentric over end-range stretching, closed-chain over open-chain in early phases)
2. **Compensatory muscle downregulation must be paired with restoration of underlying stability** — never stretch the overactive compensator (e.g., overactive upper trap) without addressing the laxity it is compensating for
3. **Set expectations based on Spanhove trajectory** — 6-month minimum, progressive improvement, WOSI/DASH/PSFS as primary outcomes
4. **Address kinesiophobia separately** — exercise alone does not modify TSK per Spanhove (p=0.12)
5. **Refer for medical specialist evaluation** if multi-system features suggest hEDS or syndromic hypermobility (autonomic dysregulation, GI motility, cardiovascular features, etc.)

### What This Decision Logic Does NOT Authorize

- **Standard mobility-first sequencing in hypermobile cases** — this is contraindicated; the case's primary deficit is stability, not mobility
- **Aggressive end-range stretching of overactive compensators** — without restoring underlying stability, this reduces the case's only available stabilization mechanism (the chronically tight muscle)
- **Skipping the hypermobility screen** — applying non-hypermobility-aware records to a hypermobile case risks worsening the case (HL-09 risk: applying inadequately grounded interventions)
- **Generalizing Spanhove's 21-patient findings to non-hEDS/HSD populations without disclosure** — the population is specifically hEDS/HSD with multidirectional instability; other hypermobility presentations may have different responses
- **Surfacing this citation in default M1 outputs** — citations remain off by default per CLAUDE.md Section 7

## Constraint Candidates

- **CC-26.** Single RCT, n=21 → Confidence Medium. Treat the WOSI 240/325-point trajectory as "robust expected response magnitude" rather than "exact magnitude prediction for any individual case"
- **CC-27.** Spanhove studied hEDS/HSD with multidirectional instability specifically. Generalization to other hypermobility presentations (asymmetric laxity, post-traumatic acquired laxity, generalized hypermobility without instability) is inference, not direct evidence. Disclose population in any caption use
- **CC-28.** Diagnostic differentiation between hEDS, HSD, generalized joint hypermobility, asymmetric laxity, and connective tissue disorder requires separate clinical assessment (Beighton scoring, family history, multi-system features, formal genetic/specialist evaluation when warranted) — research-008 is rehabilitation evidence, not diagnostic evidence
- **CC-29.** Multi-system features suggesting syndromic hypermobility (autonomic dysregulation, GI motility issues, cardiovascular features, easy bruising, easy fatigue) warrant **medical specialist referral**, not rehabilitation alone — joint-only management is insufficient
- **CC-30.** Spanhove found NO between-group difference between two home-based program variants — do not authorize claims that one specific protocol is superior. The broad category of structured home-based exercise produces the documented effects
- **CC-31.** **Kinesiophobia is not modified by exercise alone** (TSK p=0.12). Cases with significant fear-of-movement need separate cognitive/educational intervention — exercise programming alone is insufficient
- **CC-32.** Spanhove's adult hEDS/HSD population does not extend to pediatric hypermobility, generalized non-hEDS hypermobility without instability, post-surgical instability, or acute traumatic dislocation — these populations may have different responses

## Confidence Level

**Medium.** Single RCT, n=21, two-arm with no between-group winner. Strengths: direct case-population match (hEDS/HSD with multidirectional instability); multiple validated outcome measures with verifiable exact figures; 6-month follow-up; clinically practical home-based intervention. Limitations: small sample, single study, no sham control, two-arm with no between-group differentiation, single journal.

Promotion to High requires:
- A meta-analysis or NMA on hypermobility-specific shoulder instability rehab (does not appear to exist yet)
- ≥ 1 dual-stream Phase 2 hypermobile case demonstrating WOSI improvement trajectory consistent with Spanhove (clinical replication)
- Larger RCTs with sham controls

This is the practical reality of the hypermobility-specific literature. Spanhove is the strongest available seed.
