# QUERY 006 — TMJ Subluxation / Dislocation Chronic Instability and Right-Side Jaw Dysfunction

This query was logged 2026-05-01 from a real personal case as a fourth follow-on to query-001 (added in v4). It isolates the **right TMJ subluxation/dislocation history and chronic instability layer** that is anatomically distinct from research-001 (cervical/thoracic), research-002 (cervicothoracic nerve root), research-003 (regional CS), and research-004 (scapulothoracic). ## Use Case

A right-side TMJ chronic dysfunction history with a clear progression pattern:

1. **Onset:** Habitual tic — moving the mouth to the right side, pulling the right TMJ. This was a recurring movement pattern, not a single event. Produced **tiny clicks** during the tic — possibly representing micro-tears in soft tissue (capsular, ligamentous, or disc-derangement micro-events).

2. **First larger event:** During one of these movements, the jaw moved fast and felt good — a release-type sensation. This is consistent with a self-reduction event of partial subluxation.

3. **Second event (the structural failure):** Under significant stress, after an aggressive mouth movement, the jaw **snapped**. The jaw was then "hanging and rubber-like" — overextended, unstable. This is consistent with an acute traumatic dislocation or substantial subluxation event with capsular/disc derangement.

4. **Chronic post-event state (current):**
   - Jaw deviates more to the left every time the mouth opens too wide on the right side (consistent with disrupted right-side TMJ mechanics, possibly disc displacement without reduction, capsular laxity, or compensatory left-side bias)
   - Persistent pain on the right at the **ear-to-cheek connection** and **under the jaw** (anatomical region matches the TMJ joint capsule and surrounding masticatory soft tissue)
   - **Sensation of "something always disconnected"** — patient-reported subjective marker for chronic capsular/disc instability
   - On swallowing: something moves inward then outward — pressure/inflammation feel. This is consistent with chronic disc derangement or capsular dysfunction interacting with the swallowing mechanism (the digastric, suprahyoid, and adjacent musculature engage with each swallow and may compress/relieve the affected joint)

5. **Diagnostic uncertainty:** Self-hypothesis is TMJ subluxation. Other lay/clinical input has agreed it might be the case. **No formal clinical diagnosis exists.** The differential includes:
   - TMJ subluxation (recurrent, self-reducing)
   - TMJ dislocation (one-time event with chronic sequelae)
   - Anterior disc displacement without reduction (ADDwoR)
   - Capsular instability / chronic capsulitis
   - Combination of the above
   - Resolving these requires clinical examination + likely MRI

## Core Question

What does the literature establish about:

1. The diagnostic differentiation between TMJ subluxation, dislocation, and chronic disc derangement in patients with a clear traumatic injury history and chronic right-dominant jaw symptoms?
2. Conservative vs. surgical management outcomes for chronic TMJ instability without acute reducible dislocation?
3. The role of habitual movement patterns (tics, parafunctional behaviors, bruxism) as both trigger and maintenance mechanism for TMJ dysfunction?
4. The relationship between TMJ dysfunction and adjacent cervicothoracic chain dysfunction — is the right-side TMJ involvement causal, consequential, or co-developmental with the broader right-dominant chain pattern in this case?
