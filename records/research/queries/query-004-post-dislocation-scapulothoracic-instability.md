# QUERY 004 — Post-Dislocation Scapulothoracic Instability and Compensatory Splinting

This query was authored from a real personal case as a fourth follow-on to query-001 (the case's right-shoulder-blade dislocation history). It isolates the **chronic post-dislocation instability + scapulothoracic dysfunction layer** that research-001 (cervical/thoracic mechanical), research-002 (cervicothoracic nerve root), and research-003 (regional central sensitization) do not directly cover. Anatomically distinct from research-006 (TMJ — same case, different joint).

---

## Use Case

A chronic post-dislocation shoulder pattern presenting as a compounding factor in the broader right-dominant chain:

- **Injury history:** **Two prior dislocations at the right shoulder blade region** (scapulothoracic / glenohumeral region — exact anatomical specification not yet clinically confirmed; could be glenohumeral, AC, or scapulothoracic involvement).
- **Current chronic state:**
  - Right shoulder blade region remains a primary symptomatic site within the broader right posterior chain
  - Suspected chronic scapular dyskinesis and/or compensatory muscle splinting around the affected region
  - Symptoms wrap into rear deltoid + subscapular regions during query-001 flares
  - Provocation/relief patterns from query-001 (sustained flexion provokes; extension + scapular retraction relieves) are *especially pronounced* at the right shoulder blade region — suggesting the dislocation history compounded mechanical sensitivity in this region
- **Compounding interaction:** the post-dislocation instability layer almost certainly amplifies the central sensitization layer (research-003) and may contribute to the regional symptom spreading pattern. A chronically unstable joint produces chronic peripheral nociceptive input → upregulates central sensitization → makes mechanical compensation more persistent.

---

## Core Question

What does the literature establish about:

1. The **conservative management evidence base** for chronic post-dislocation shoulder instability with compensatory scapulothoracic dysfunction?
2. The **rehabilitation outcomes** for scapular dyskinesis as a downstream effect of dislocation/instability events?
3. The **decision threshold** for conservative trial vs. surgical referral in chronic recurrent instability cases?

Sub-questions:
- What rehabilitation duration + content is evidenced for chronic post-dislocation cases?
- What is the recurrence rate after conservative care vs. surgery in chronic instability?
- What scapular dyskinesis screening identifies cases needing rehabilitation focus?
- How does post-dislocation scapulothoracic dysfunction interact with broader chain dysfunction (research-001 + research-003 patterns)?

---

## Mechanism Focus

- Glenohumeral capsular and ligamentous laxity post-dislocation
- Scapulothoracic dyskinesis — altered scapular positioning and motion
- Rotator cuff and scapular stabilizer dysfunction
- Chronic compensatory muscle splinting / co-contraction around unstable joint
- Kinetic chain integration (scapula ↔ humerus ↔ trunk ↔ legs)
- Peripheral nociceptive input from chronic instability as upstream driver of central sensitization

---

## Decision Target

This query must inform:

1. When a chronic post-dislocation case with scapular dyskinesis warrants a **structured rehabilitation trial** (duration, components) before considering surgical referral
2. Reassessment markers for scapular dyskinesis resolution under rehabilitation
3. The interaction between post-dislocation rehabilitation (this record) and the broader case sequencing (research-001 mechanical + research-003 combined-modality + research-002 inflammation)
4. Red flags that supersede conservative trial (high recurrence frequency, motor weakness, neural deficits, severe instability with daily-life impact)

---

## Constraints

- Acute reducible shoulder dislocation requires immediate medical reduction — different management from chronic post-dislocation rehabilitation
- High-frequency recurrent dislocation (multiple events per year) likely warrants surgical evaluation, not extended conservative trial
- Specific anatomical lesions (Bankart, Hill-Sachs, posterior labral tear, etc.) require imaging confirmation and may modify rehabilitation expectations
- Findings on glenohumeral instability rehabilitation may not extend cleanly to AC joint or scapulothoracic primary pathology — distinguish anatomical specificity
- Do not surface citations in default M1 outputs — only when significantly informative or explicitly requested
- Do not fabricate PMIDs or invent figures — verify direct against PubMed

## Resolution

Status: **resolved** — 2026-05-01.

### Seed Record Locked

**research-004** — Carbone S, Postacchini R, Gumina S. 2015, Knee Surgery, Sports Traumatology, Arthroscopy. **PMID: 24458335.**

*Scapular dyskinesis and SICK syndrome in patients with a chronic type III acromioclavicular dislocation. Results of rehabilitation.*

Captured at `records/research/captured/research-004-post-dislocation-scapular-dyskinesis-rehabilitation.md`.
Mapped at `records/research/mapped/research-004-post-dislocation-scapular-dyskinesis-rehabilitation.md`.

### Verified Figures from Abstract

- **Scapular dyskinesis resolved in 18/23 patients (78.2%) at 12 months**
- **Mean Constant Score and Subjective Shoulder Value: improved to 85 points** at 12 months
- **SICK Scapula Rating Scale: 7.5 points** in 4 patients post-treatment
- **SICK syndrome observed in 4/8 patients with scapular malposition** post-treatment
- **Sample:** 24 patients enrolled, 23 analyzed at 12-month follow-up; 14/24 had SICK syndrome at baseline
- **Intervention:** 12 strengthening and stretching exercises of the scapulae
- **Follow-up:** 6 weeks, 6 months, 12 months
- **Level of Evidence: IV**

### How the Resolution Answers the Query

- **Mechanism:** Carbone provides direct evidence that chronic post-dislocation scapular dyskinesis is responsive to structured scapular-focused rehabilitation. The 78.2% resolution figure at 12 months establishes a population-scale expectation for rehabilitation responsiveness in chronic post-dislocation cases.
- **Conservative trial framework:** 12 specific scapular exercises over a multi-month program (with 6-week, 6-month, and 12-month checkpoints) is the empirical anchor.
- **Decision threshold:** Cases without red flags (high-frequency recurrent dislocation, severe daily-life impact, motor weakness, neural deficits, imaging-confirmed major lesions) can reasonably enter a 12-week+ structured trial before considering surgical referral.
- **Cross-record sequencing:** When research-004 post-dislocation case AND research-003 CS features both present → run combined-modality program incorporating scapular-focused work as a component (RC-14).

### Disclosure

Carbone's population is **type III acromioclavicular (AC) dislocation specifically** — not glenohumeral, scapulothoracic, or other dislocation types. Generalization to other dislocation populations is **inference, not direct evidence** (CC-15). Confidence Medium (Level IV evidence, n=24, AC-specific). **research-007 (planned)** flagged for broader-population supporting evidence to elevate confidence to High.
