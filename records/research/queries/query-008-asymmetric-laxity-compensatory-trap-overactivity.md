## Use Case

A constitutional clinical pattern combining passive instability and active compensation, both right-dominant:

### Layer 1 — Unilateral Right-Side Laxity (the passive substrate)

- Patient self-reports the entire right side of the body is "very lax"
- Explicitly identified at: right shoulder blade, right jaw, right upper trap region
- Asymmetric — the contralateral side does not show the same laxity (uncommon presentation)
- Predisposes to acute injury under load (consistent with the case's two prior right scapular dislocations, right TMJ "snap" event, C-T junction injury under intense breathing)
- Predisposes to chronic compensation when stability is structurally inadequate

### Layer 2 — Compensatory Upper Trap Overactivity (the active response)

- Right-dominant overactive upper trap with very tight right-side neck
- Forward-lean / curved-back / bad posture provokes the upper trap to "ride" up across the whole top of the body on the right side
- Right upper trap described as overactive, tight, inflamed during flares
- This is the **textbook compensatory response to underlying lax shoulder/scapular mechanics** — the surrounding muscles chronically over-activate to provide the passive stability the lax joint can't deliver

### The Combined Picture

Layer 1 + Layer 2 = **right-dominant hyperlaxity + compensatory upper trap dominance**, a classical presentation in the hypermobility/instability literature.

## Constraints

- Not all hypermobility is pathological — generalized joint hypermobility (GJH) without symptoms or instability is a benign variant; hypermobility spectrum disorder (HSD) and hypermobile EDS (hEDS) are clinical entities with diagnostic criteria
- Asymmetric laxity is uncommon — formal clinical assessment is needed to differentiate from generalized hypermobility expressed asymmetrically
- Hypermobility-specific rehab is not just "less aggressive" rehab — the principles are different (stability, proprioception, neuromuscular control, eccentric loading)
- Treating compensatory muscle overactivity with stretching alone, without addressing underlying laxity, may reduce the only available stability mechanism — potentially worsening outcomes
- Connective tissue disorders may have multi-system features (cardiovascular, autonomic, GI) — joint-only management is insufficient when systemic features are present

*Home-based exercise therapy for treating shoulder instability in patients with hypermobile Ehlers-Danlos syndrome/hypermobility spectrum disorders. A randomized trial.*

Captured at `records/research/captured/research-008-hypermobility-shoulder-instability-rehabilitation.md`.

### Verified Figures from Abstract

- **Sample:** 21 hEDS/HSD patients with multidirectional shoulder instability
- **Intervention:** 6-month home-based exercise program (two arms; no significant between-group difference)
- **WOSI:** improved 240 points at 12 weeks (p = 0.02); 325 points at 24 weeks (p = 0.001)
- **DASH:** 8.6 point improvement at 24 weeks (p = 0.002)
- **PSFS:** 4.3 point improvement at 24 weeks (p = 0.01)
- **GROC:** 1.02 point improvement at 24 weeks (p = 0.001)
- **TSK:** no significant effect (p = 0.12)

### How the Resolution Answers the Query

- **Diagnostic differentiation:** Spanhove studied an already-diagnosed hEDS/HSD population. Diagnostic differentiation (asymmetric laxity vs. generalized hypermobility vs. hEDS vs. HSD) requires separate clinical assessment instruments (Beighton scoring, family history, multi-system features). Research-008 is rehabilitation evidence, not diagnostic evidence — the diagnostic question requires clinician workup outside the scope of this record.
- **Rehabilitation approach:** The horizontal-modifier principles (stability over flexibility, proprioception over passive range, slow eccentric over end-range stretching, closed-chain over open-chain in early phases, never stretch the overactive compensator without restoring underlying stability) are now grounded as cross-record modifications to research-001/002/003/004/005 interventions for hypermobile cases.
- **Outcomes data:** WOSI 240→325 point improvement trajectory across 12–24 weeks provides expected response anchors. Multiple validated outcome measures (DASH, PSFS, GROC) confirm clinically meaningful gains.
- **Stretching of overactive compensators:** While Spanhove doesn't directly test stretching as harm, the broader principle (don't reduce the case's only available stability mechanism without restoring underlying stability) is captured in CC-26 through CC-32. Future research could address this directly.
- **Kinesiophobia is not modified by exercise alone** (TSK p=0.12) — important finding indicating that fear-of-movement requires separate intervention class.

### Disclosure

Confidence Medium. Single RCT, n=21, two-arm with no between-group winner, no sham control, single journal. The hypermobility-specific shoulder instability literature has small sample sizes and no NMA-level evidence yet. Spanhove is the strongest available seed despite limitations.

Instead, it modifies how research-001/002/003/004/005 interventions should be applied when the hypermobility screen is positive. The case engine should screen for hypermobility *before* applying mechanism-specific record sequencing.

## Note on Constitutional Layer Significance

In a properly populated system, the constitutional layer should be screened *first* during case classification — before applying any specific record's sequencing. A case with hypermobility/laxity substrate needs hypermobility-aware versions of the research-001, -002, -003, -004, -005 interventions. Without that adjustment, evidence-based interventions designed for non-hypermobile populations may be applied incorrectly to a hypermobile patient.

This argues for research-008's eventual position in the system as a **horizontal modifier** rather than another vertical mechanism layer — analogous to how red-flag screening operates across all case categories.
